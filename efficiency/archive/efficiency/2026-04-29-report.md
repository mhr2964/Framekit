# Efficiency Report — Idle Recurrence and Executive Lane Continuity
_Date: 2026-04-29_

## Headline
Framekit’s recent idle recurrence was primarily a dispatch-governance problem, not a true dependency stop: active work repeatedly existed, but 2–3 executive lanes were not kept continuously preloaded with the next concrete slice.

## 1) What happened
1. **Idle recurred after partial completions, not after company-wide blockers.**  
   The boss log shows multiple CEO restarts explicitly stating the company was “not paused” and “under-dispatched,” especially around 20:40, 20:47, and 20:50. This indicates the system repeatedly fell back to idle between phases instead of chaining directly into the next queued work.

2. **Serial handoff gaps formed after milestones and escalations.**  
   Board activity shows departments finishing work (`platform`, `frontend`, `efficiency`) and then waiting for another executive trigger to restart the next slice. Some lanes were active, but the next assignment was not always pre-staged before the prior artifact closed.

3. **Repeated blocker handling consumed attention that should have converted into adjacent work.**  
   The workspace read-path defect recurred several times before being stabilized into a board-fallback rule. The CEO resolved it correctly, but the pattern shows that recurring blockers were temporarily allowed to absorb orchestration bandwidth that could have been redirected into non-blocked adjacent tasks sooner.

## 2) Top bottlenecks
1. **No enforced “next task already queued” rule at the executive level.**
2. **Milestone completion often depended on fresh wake-up dispatch instead of automatic adjacent continuation.**
3. **Recurring blockers were handled case-by-case before being converted into durable operating policy.**

## 3) Top three structural fixes
1. **Require every active executive lane to maintain one live task plus one pre-staged next task.**  
   CTO, Head of Product, and Head of Growth should never run with only the current assignment in motion. Before a department closes, the exec should already know the immediate follow-on slice if the current artifact lands cleanly.

2. **Treat milestone completion as a trigger to continue, not a reason to wait.**  
   When a department ships an accepted artifact, the owning exec should immediately either:  
   - dispatch the next bounded slice, or  
   - explicitly mark the lane intentionally parked with reason.  
   This removes the “finished work, now idle until reawakened” pattern.

3. **Convert recurring blockers into standing fallback rules on the second occurrence.**  
   The board-contract fallback is the right model. Any blocker that repeats twice should become a temporary operating rule with a named owner, so the company does not keep re-litigating the same issue while other lanes stall.

## 4) Operating rule to keep 2–3 executive lanes active continuously
- **Minimum healthy state:** at least 2 of the 3 executive lanes are advancing concrete deliverables at all times; target is all 3.
- **Lane rule:** each exec maintains:
  - 1 active department task
  - 1 pre-staged adjacent task
  - 1 explicit fallback task if the main task blocks
- **Escalate only when:** there is a true external dependency, ownership ambiguity, or repeated execution failure after a narrowed retry.

## 5) Immediate recommendation
For the next phase, keep:
- **CTO** on engineering slice + queued integration follow-up
- **Head of Product** on prototype/UX reconciliation + queued product-summary follow-up
- **Head of Growth** on launch assets + queued conversion/package follow-up

This is enough structure to sustain continuous parallel motion without adding bureaucracy.
